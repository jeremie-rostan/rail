    EU AI Act Alignment

    EU AI Act high-risk uses are prohibited at ISP*

    Deployers and providers obligations are built into the categories, prevention measures, and responses above.


    * Based on the EU AI Act, here is the list of Prohibited and High-Risk AI uses specifically relevant to education.

    Prohibited AI Practices (Banned in Schools)

    These uses pose an unacceptable risk and are strictly forbidden:


    • Emotion Recognition: Using AI to infer emotions or intentions of students or staff (e.g., to measure engagement or attention), unless strictly for medical or safety reasons.
    • Behavioral Manipulation: Using AI to deploy subliminal techniques or exploit vulnerabilities (such as age or disability) to distort behavior in a way that causes harm.
    • Social Scoring: Evaluating or classifying students based on social behavior or personality traits that leads to detrimental treatment.
    • Biometric Categorization: Categorizing natural persons to deduce sensitive attributes (e.g., race, political opinions, religious beliefs, sexual orientation) based on biometric data.

    High-Risk AI Systems (Strictly Regulated)

    These uses are permitted but subject to heavy compliance obligations (assessments, human oversight, accuracy, logs):


    • Admissions & Assignment: Determining access or admission to educational institutions, or assigning students to specific educational levels or programs.
    • Evaluation & Grading: Evaluating learning outcomes (grading) or using AI to steer the learning process (adaptive learning) based on student data.
    • Proctoring: Monitoring and detecting prohibited behavior of students during tests.
    • Staff Management: Recruitment, selection, task allocation, or performance evaluation of teachers and school staff.

    EU AI Act Summary

    The following report outlines the obligations for K-12 schools.


    Role Definition: Under the Act, schools are primarily defined as Deployers (users of AI systems under their authority), though they may be Providers if they develop their own AI tools internally. Most obligations below apply to schools as Deployers of High-Risk AI Systems.

    1. Prohibited AI Practices (Strictly Banned)

    Schools are prohibited from using AI systems for the following purposes, as they pose unacceptable risks:


    • Emotion Recognition: Using AI to infer emotions or intentions of students or staff within educational institutions is prohibited, except for medical or safety reasons,,.
    • Behavioral Manipulation: AI using subliminal techniques or exploiting vulnerabilities (e.g., age or disability) to distort behavior in a way that causes harm,.
    • Social Scoring: Evaluating or classifying students based on social behavior or personal traits that leads to detrimental treatment,.
    • Biometric Categorization: Categorizing individuals to deduce sensitive attributes (e.g., race, political opinion, religious beliefs),.

    2. High-Risk AI Classifications in Education

    Schools must identify if they are using "High-Risk" systems. Annex III explicitly classifies AI as high-risk if used for,,:


    • Determining access or admission to educational institutions.
    • Assigning students to educational levels or programs.
    • Evaluating learning outcomes (grading/assessment).
    • Steering the learning process (adaptive learning) based on student outcomes.
    • Monitoring and detecting prohibited behavior during tests (AI proctoring).
    • Recruitment and evaluation of staff/teachers,.

    3. Obligations for Schools (as Deployers of High-Risk AI)

    If a school deploys a High-Risk AI system, it must comply with the following Article 26 obligations:


    • Technical Compliance: Use the system strictly in accordance with the instructions for use provided by the developer.
    • Human Oversight: Assign natural persons (staff) to oversee the AI. These individuals must be competent, properly trained, and have the authority to intervene or stop the system,.
    • Input Data Governance: If the school controls input data, it must ensure the data is relevant and sufficiently representative for the intended purpose.
    • Monitoring & Incident Reporting: Monitor the system's operation. If a risk presents itself, the school must suspend use and inform the provider/distributor. Serious incidents must be reported to the provider and market surveillance authorities,.
    • Logs: Keep automatically generated system logs for at least six months (or longer if required by data protection laws),.
    • Transparency with Staff: Inform workers (teachers/staff) and their representatives before putting a high-risk AI system into service at the workplace.
    • Transparency with Students: Inform natural persons (students/parents) if they are subject to decisions made or assisted by high-risk AI.

    4. Specific Obligations for Public Authorities

    As "bodies governed by public law," most schools have additional requirements:


    • Registration: Schools must register themselves and the high-risk system they use in the EU Database before putting it into service,.
    • Fundamental Rights Impact Assessment (FRIA): Prior to deploying a high-risk system, schools must assess the potential impact on fundamental rights (e.g., right to education, non-discrimination, data protection). This assessment must be notified to the national market surveillance authority.

    5. AI Literacy (Article 4)

    Schools must ensure a sufficient level of AI Literacy for their staff and other persons dealing with the operation and use of AI systems. This includes training on technical knowledge, experience, and the context in which the systems are used,.

    6. Transparency for General Purpose AI

    If schools use chatbots or systems that generate "deep fake" content (images, audio, video), they must:


    • Ensure that natural persons know they are interacting with an AI (unless obvious).
    • Disclose if content has been artificially generated or manipulated,.

    7. Implementation Timeline

    • February 2, 2025: Prohibitions (e.g., emotion recognition bans) and AI Literacy rules apply,.
    • August 2, 2026: Obligations for High-Risk AI systems (monitoring, FRIA, registration) enter into force,.

    Based on the EU AI Act, a K-12 school is classified as a Provider if it develops an AI system (internally or has it developed by a third party) and places it on the market or puts it into service under its own name or trademark, whether for payment or free of charge,.


    This distinction is critical because Providers bear the heaviest regulatory burden under the Act.

    1. When is a School Considered a "Provider"?

    A school moves from being a "Deployer" (user) to a "Provider" (developer/owner) in the following scenarios:


    • Internal Development: The school’s IT department or students build an AI tool (e.g., an admissions algorithm or a grading bot) and the school uses it for its operations.
    • "White Labeling": The school commissions a third-party developer to build a custom AI tool and releases or uses it under the school’s name.
    • Substantial Modification: The school modifies an existing third-party AI system (e.g., changing the code or training data of a commercial tool) in a way that affects its compliance or changes its intended purpose,.
    • Purpose Shift: The school modifies a non-high-risk system (e.g., a general spreadsheet tool) to function as a high-risk system (e.g., to predict student dropout rates).

    2. Obligations for Schools as Providers of High-Risk AI

    If a school develops a system falling under Annex III (e.g., for admissions, assigning students to educational levels, evaluating learning outcomes, or monitoring prohibited behavior during tests),, it is a Provider of a High-Risk AI System. The school must comply with Articles 8–25, which include:


    • Risk Management System: Establish a continuous system to identify and mitigate risks to health, safety, and fundamental rights throughout the system's lifecycle,,.
    • Data Governance: Ensure training, validation, and testing datasets are relevant, representative, free of errors, and complete. This includes checking for and mitigating bias (e.g., preventing discrimination against specific student demographics),,.
    • Technical Documentation: Create and maintain detailed documentation demonstrating compliance, which must be kept for 10 years,,.
    • Record-Keeping (Logs): Design the system to automatically record events (logs) to ensure traceability of the system’s functioning,,.
    • Transparency & Instructions: Create detailed instructions for use, explaining the system’s characteristics, capabilities, and limitations to anyone operating it,,.
    • Human Oversight Measures: Design the system with built-in interfaces allowing natural persons (staff) to effectively oversee, intervene, or stop the system,,.
    • Accuracy, Robustness & Cybersecurity: Ensure the system achieves appropriate levels of accuracy and is resilient against errors or malicious attacks (e.g., data poisoning),,.
    • Quality Management System (QMS): Implement a documented QMS covering regulatory compliance, design control, and post-market monitoring,,.
    • Conformity Assessment: Perform an assessment (internal or third-party) to demonstrate the system meets all requirements before using it,,.
    • Registration: Register the high-risk AI system in the EU Database,,.
    • CE Marking: Affix the CE marking to the system to indicate conformity,,.

    3. General Purpose AI (GPAI) Models

    If a school develops a General Purpose AI Model (an AI capable of performing a wide range of distinct tasks, like a large language model),, it has specific provider obligations, even if the model is not classified as high-risk:


    • Technical Documentation: Maintain detailed documentation on the model’s training and testing,.
    • Copyright Policy: Put in place a policy to comply with Union copyright law,.
    • Training Data Summary: Publish a detailed summary of the content used to train the model,.


    Note: If the model presents a systemic risk (e.g., high-impact capabilities), the school must also perform adversarial testing ("red teaming"), assess systemic risks, and report serious incidents to the AI Office,.

    4. Post-Market Monitoring & Reporting

    Even after the system is in use, the school (as provider) must:


    • Monitor Performance: Actively collect and analyze data on the system's performance in real-world settings,.
    • Report Incidents: Immediately report any "serious incident" (e.g., breach of fundamental rights, harm to health) to the national market surveillance authority,.
    • Corrective Actions: Immediately withdraw, disable, or recall the system if it is found to be non-compliant,.

    5. Research Exemption

    The Regulation does not apply to AI systems developed and put into service solely for the purpose of scientific research and development,. However, this exemption is strict; if the system is used for actual educational decision-making or student assessment (outside of the research scope), the obligations listed above apply.

    The Data Protection Principles


    1️⃣ Lawfulness, Fairness & Transparency

    Lawfulness You must have a valid legal basis (Public Task, Legal Obligation, etc.).


    Fairness Don’t use data in ways that are unexpected, misleading, or harmful.


    Transparency Tell people clearly:


    • What data you collect
    • Why you collect it
    • How it’s used
    • Who it’s shared with
    • How long it’s kept


    👉 In schools: update privacy notices when introducing AI tools.



    2️⃣ Purpose Limitation

    Only collect data for specified, explicit purposes.


    You cannot later use it for something incompatible.


    Example:


    • Attendance data collected for safeguarding cannot later be used for marketing.
    • Student essays uploaded for marking should not be reused to train AI models (unless lawfully agreed).



    3️⃣ Data Minimisation

    Only collect what is necessary and proportionate.


    Ask:


    • Do we need full names?
    • Can we use ID numbers?
    • Do we need historical data?
    • Can we anonymise?


    AI systems often encourage over-collection — this principle pushes back against that.



    4️⃣ Accuracy

    Personal data must be accurate and kept up to date.


    In AI contexts:


    • Monitor for incorrect outputs
    • Allow correction of errors
    • Avoid automated assumptions becoming “truth”


    If an AI flags a pupil as “high risk,” that must be reviewable and correctable.



    5️⃣ Storage Limitation

    Keep data no longer than necessary.


    Best practice:


    • Defined retention schedule
    • Automatic deletion where possible
    • Vendor deletion agreements
    • Annual data audits


    AI systems often retain prompts by default — check this.



    6️⃣ Integrity & Confidentiality (Security)

    Protect data against:


    • Unauthorised access
    • Loss
    • Destruction
    • Damage


    Best practices:


    • Encryption
    • Access controls
    • MFA
    • Audit logs
    • Staff training
    • Secure vendor agreements


    With AI tools, also ensure:


    • No public model training on your data
    • No open sharing of prompts with identifiable information



    7️⃣ Accountability

    This is the principle many people forget.


    You must not only comply — you must be able to demonstrate compliance.


    That means:


    • DPIAs documented
    • Lawful basis recorded
    • Contracts in place
    • Policies written
    • Training delivered
    • Decisions justified


    If you cannot evidence it, legally it may as well not exist.

    AI and GDPR

    Best practices for complying with the GDPR and broader data protection requirements when using AI tools in education.



    1. Establish Lawful Basis for Processing

    Under the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), schools must identify a lawful basis before processing personal data.

    ✔ Common lawful bases in schools:

    • Public task (most common for state schools)
    • Legal obligation
    • Consent (use carefully – must be freely given and withdrawable)
    • Legitimate interests (more common for independent schools)


    ⚠️ Do not rely on consent where there is an imbalance of power (e.g., compulsory classroom tools).



    2. Conduct a Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA)

    AI tools often involve:


    • Automated decision-making
    • Profiling
    • Processing children’s data (high-risk category)


    A DPIA is mandatory when risk is high.

    Your DPIA should assess:

    • What data is collected?
    • Is sensitive data involved? (SEN status, health, behaviour)
    • Is there automated decision-making?
    • Could the AI produce bias or unfair outcomes?
    • How long is data stored?
    • Where is data stored (UK/EU or third country)?



    3. Minimise Data (Data Minimisation Principle)

    Only collect what is strictly necessary.

    Best practice:

    • Avoid uploading full class lists if not required
    • Use pseudonymised IDs where possible
    • Turn off unnecessary tracking features
    • Disable data sharing with third parties


    AI tools should not become a “data lake” of student information.



    4. Verify AI Vendor Compliance

    Before adopting any AI tool:

    Check the vendor:

    • Has a clear privacy policy


    • Signs a Data Processing Agreement (DPA)


    • Provides information on:


      • Data storage location
      • Sub-processors
      • Security measures
      • Data retention


    • Does not use student data to train models (unless explicitly agreed)


    If data leaves the UK/EU, ensure:


    • Adequacy decision OR
    • Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs)



    5. Ensure Transparency

    Under GDPR transparency rules:

    Schools must:

    • Update privacy notices to mention AI use


    • Explain:


      • What AI is used for
      • What data is processed
      • Whether decisions are automated


    • Use age-appropriate explanations for students


    Parents and students must understand what is happening with their data.



    6. Protect Children’s Data (Enhanced Safeguards)

    Children’s data requires extra protection.


    Best practice:


    • Avoid fully automated decision-making affecting pupils
    • Always involve a human in important decisions
    • Monitor for algorithmic bias
    • Prevent profiling that could disadvantage vulnerable students


    Automated grading or behaviour scoring must have human oversight.



    7. Strengthen Security Controls

    AI increases cyber risk.

    Implement:

    • Role-based access controls
    • Multi-factor authentication
    • Staff access logging
    • Regular audits
    • Encrypted storage
    • Secure deletion procedures


    Ensure AI tools integrate with existing school security systems safely.



    8. Staff Training and Governance

    Most GDPR breaches happen due to human error.

    Train staff on:

    • What data can be uploaded to AI tools
    • What must NEVER be uploaded (e.g., safeguarding notes)
    • Prompt hygiene (avoid identifiable data in public AI systems)
    • Recognising AI hallucinations and bias


    Appoint a responsible lead (usually the DPO or SLT member).



    9. Avoid Unlawful Automated Decision-Making

    Under GDPR Article 22: Individuals have the right not to be subject solely to automated decisions that have legal or significant effects.


    Schools must:


    • Ensure no purely automated disciplinary or academic decisions
    • Provide clear appeal mechanisms
    • Include human review



    10. Create an AI Usage Policy

    Develop a formal policy covering:


    • Approved AI tools
    • Prohibited uses
    • Staff and student responsibilities
    • Data protection requirements
    • Safeguarding alignment
    • Monitoring and review procedures


    Integrate with:


    • Safeguarding policy
    • Behaviour policy
    • ICT acceptable use policy



    11. Manage Data Retention and Deletion

    AI vendors may store prompts and outputs.


    Ensure:


    • Defined retention schedules
    • Right to erasure procedures
    • Account deletion processes
    • Backup deletion confirmation


    Test deletion processes periodically.



    12. Maintain Ongoing Monitoring

    AI compliance is not one-time.


    Schedule:


    • Annual DPIA review
    • Vendor compliance review
    • Policy updates
    • Bias and fairness audits
    • Security penetration testing

    1️⃣ You Need Two Legal Bases (Not One)

    When processing special category data, you must have:


    • A lawful basis under Article 6 (Public Task, Legal Obligation, etc.)
    • An additional condition under Article 9


    Most schools forget the second step.



    2️⃣ Common Article 9 Conditions for Schools

    A. Substantial Public Interest (Most Common for State Schools)

    Used when processing is necessary for safeguarding, equality monitoring, or SEN provision.


    Examples:


    • AI identifying safeguarding risks
    • Systems analysing attendance patterns linked to vulnerability
    • Recording SEN adjustments
    • Equality monitoring dashboards


    You must:


    • Have an appropriate policy document
    • Retain it
    • Document safeguards



    B. Education and Social Protection Law

    Often overlaps with substantial public interest.


    Used for:


    • SEN support
    • Safeguarding
    • Welfare support systems



    C. Explicit Consent (High Bar)

    Only appropriate when:


    • The activity is genuinely optional
    • Consent is explicit (clear statement)
    • It can be withdrawn without consequences


    Example:


    • Voluntary wellbeing AI survey
    • Optional biometric access system


    ⚠️ In compulsory education settings, explicit consent is difficult to rely on.



    3️⃣ AI Increases Risk with Special Category Data

    AI tools may:


    • Infer health conditions
    • Predict behavioural risks
    • Generate profiles
    • Detect emotional states
    • Analyse free-text disclosures


    Even if you don’t directly upload special category data, AI may infer it — which still counts.


    Example: If an AI tool flags:


    • “Likely ADHD traits”
    • “High anxiety indicators”
    • “Behavioural risk student”


    You are now processing special category data.

    Your DPIA must assess:


    • Bias risk
    • Profiling impact
    • False positives
    • Safeguarding implications
    • Human oversight

    If special category data is involved:

    • Human review becomes essential
    • You must provide meaningful explanation

    • Clear role-based permissions
    • Logging and monitoring
    • Shorter retention periods where possible

    You’re thinking of the seven key principles of data protection under the General Data Protection Regulation (Article 5). These form the foundation of privacy best practice in schools and any organisation using AI.


    Here they are in clear, practical terms:



    The 7 GDPR Data Protection Principles


    1️⃣ Lawfulness, Fairness & Transparency

    Lawfulness You must have a valid legal basis (Public Task, Legal Obligation, etc.).


    Fairness Don’t use data in ways that are unexpected, misleading, or harmful.


    Transparency Tell people clearly:


    • What data you collect
    • Why you collect it
    • How it’s used
    • Who it’s shared with
    • How long it’s kept


    👉 In schools: update privacy notices when introducing AI tools.



    2️⃣ Purpose Limitation

    Only collect data for specified, explicit purposes.


    You cannot later use it for something incompatible.


    Example:


    • Attendance data collected for safeguarding cannot later be used for marketing.
    • Student essays uploaded for marking should not be reused to train AI models (unless lawfully agreed).



    3️⃣ Data Minimisation

    Only collect what is necessary and proportionate.


    Ask:


    • Do we need full names?
    • Can we use ID numbers?
    • Do we need historical data?
    • Can we anonymise?


    AI systems often encourage over-collection — this principle pushes back against that.



    4️⃣ Accuracy

    Personal data must be accurate and kept up to date.


    In AI contexts:


    • Monitor for incorrect outputs
    • Allow correction of errors
    • Avoid automated assumptions becoming “truth”


    If an AI flags a pupil as “high risk,” that must be reviewable and correctable.



    5️⃣ Storage Limitation

    Keep data no longer than necessary.


    Best practice:


    • Defined retention schedule
    • Automatic deletion where possible
    • Vendor deletion agreements
    • Annual data audits


    AI systems often retain prompts by default — check this.



    6️⃣ Integrity & Confidentiality (Security)

    Protect data against:


    • Unauthorised access
    • Loss
    • Destruction
    • Damage


    Best practices:


    • Encryption
    • Access controls
    • MFA
    • Audit logs
    • Staff training
    • Secure vendor agreements


    With AI tools, also ensure:


    • No public model training on your data
    • No open sharing of prompts with identifiable information



    7️⃣ Accountability

    This is the principle many people forget.


    You must not only comply — you must be able to demonstrate compliance.


    That means:


    • DPIAs documented
    • Lawful basis recorded
    • Contracts in place
    • Policies written
    • Training delivered
    • Decisions justified


    If you cannot evidence it, legally it may as well not exist.

    Think:


    Lawful – Clear – Limited – Accurate – Secure – Short-lived – Documented


    Or more formally:


    • Lawfulness, fairness, transparency
    • Purpose limitation
    • Data minimisation
    • Accuracy
    • Storage limitation
    • Integrity & confidentiality
    • Accountability
